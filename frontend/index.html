Understanding the disease
What is Noma?

Noma — also known as Cancrum oris, or "mouth fire" in several West African languages — is a devastating gangrenous infection that destroys the soft tissue and bone of the mouth and face. It begins as a simple ulcer in the gum but can spread to consume the entire cheek within days.

The disease almost exclusively strikes children between the ages of 2 and 6 who are severely malnourished and living in extreme poverty. It is not contagious, but its conditions — malnutrition, contaminated water, weakened immunity — are endemic across sub-Saharan Africa's drylands.

What makes Noma uniquely tragic is the narrowness of the treatment window. In the first stage, a simple course of oral antibiotics costing less than one US dollar can reverse the disease entirely. By Stage 3, a child requires inpatient hospital care, IV antibiotics, and nutritional rehabilitation. By Stage 4, survival means living with permanent, severe facial disfigurement and the stigma and psychological trauma that follow.

"Noma is a disease of extreme poverty. It is the face of inequity."

— Dr. Trish Khoo, MSF oral health advisor

In 2023, the World Health Organisation formally added Noma to its list of neglected tropical diseases — a recognition that came after decades of advocacy. Despite affecting tens of thousands of children a year, it receives a fraction of the research funding directed at diseases with far smaller death tolls.

Clinical staging
The four stages of Noma — and why the window matters

The entire difference between a full recovery and a lifetime of disfigurement is time — and the ability to identify the disease before it crosses into irreversibility.

Stage 1
Acute necrotising gingivitis
Survival rate: ~99% if treated

Painful, bleeding gums with necrotic ulceration at the gum line. A single course of oral amoxicillin and metronidazole reverses the disease entirely within days.

Treatment cost: ~$0.50
Stage 2
Oedema of the cheek
Survival rate: ~85% if treated

The infection spreads to the soft tissue. Swelling appears — often painless, which can delay recognition. Hospital referral is now necessary but antibiotic therapy remains effective.

Treatment cost: ~$15–40
Stage 3
Gangrenous necrosis
Survival rate: ~50–60% if treated

A zone of blackened, dry, necrotic tissue forms on the cheek. Urgent hospital admission, IV antibiotics, wound debridement, and nutritional rehabilitation required.

Treatment cost: ~$200–800
Stage 4
Scarring and sequelae
Child has survived — with disfigurement

Tissue destruction is complete. The child faces permanent facial disfigurement, trismus, speech and eating difficulties, and years of reconstructive surgery.

Reconstructive surgery: $5,000–80,000+

The systemic problem
Why Noma keeps being forgotten

Noma is not an unsolvable disease — it is an under-prioritised one. Three structural failures keep it claiming children's lives every day.

No detection system at primary care level
Community health workers — the first and often only point of contact for rural children — receive no Noma-specific training and have no tools to identify the disease in its treatable stages. By the time a specialist sees a case, it is typically Stage 3 or 4.
Extreme geographic and infrastructure barriers
The communities most affected by Noma are in remote drylands with little or no road access, no reliable electricity, and clinic-to-patient ratios that make physical outreach impossible at scale. Digital tools that work on a basic smartphone are the only viable path.
No outbreak surveillance infrastructure
Noma clusters geographically — when one child in a village is affected, others nearby are at high risk. Without a system to aggregate case reports and identify spatial clusters in real time, outbreaks are only detected weeks after they begin.
How NomaAlert solves this
A full-stack AI early warning system — built for the field

NomaAlert is not a chatbot or a research prototype. It is a production-grade backend system with three integrated components: a computer vision AI that classifies Noma stage from a photograph, a clinical reasoning model that generates structured referral notes, and a geospatial surveillance agent that identifies outbreak clusters and fires SMS alerts automatically.

The entire system is designed around one constraint: a community health worker with a basic Android smartphone and intermittent internet access. No app to install. Submit a photograph, receive a decision in under 15 minutes. Or skip the web entirely — diagnose directly on WhatsApp.

Zero-app access
Diagnose on WhatsApp — no download, no login

For communities where internet access is intermittent and app storage is scarce, WhatsApp is already installed on virtually every smartphone. NomaAlert integrates directly with WhatsApp Business API via Photon, so a health worker can submit a case, receive a diagnosis, and get a clinic referral — all within a conversation they already know how to use.

The bot guides the health worker through a structured intake flow: age and village, reported symptoms, then a photo of the affected child. The AI pipeline runs in the background — the same Kimi K2.5 vision model and K2-Thinking reasoning pipeline — and returns the result as a WhatsApp message with stage, confidence score, clinical rationale, and nearest clinic details. Transport options are included for critical cases.

About this project
Built for a problem that deserved better tools

NomaAlert was built by a team united around a single conviction: that a disease killing tens of thousands of children every year, that can be cured for less than a dollar, should not go undetected for lack of a piece of software.

We are not a medical organisation. We are engineers and designers who built the infrastructure that a trained public health response would need: an image triage pipeline, a WhatsApp diagnostic channel, a surveillance system, and a referral workflow that reaches the health worker wherever they are.

The backend runs on a 3-VM AI pipeline using Kimi K2.5 for vision classification and Kimi K2-Thinking for clinical reasoning, backed by Supabase for geospatial case storage, Twilio for SMS delivery, and Photon for WhatsApp Business API integration.

We acknowledge the foundational work of the Noma Foundation (Amsterdam), Médecins Sans Frontières, Operation Smile, and Facing Africa — organisations who have spent decades treating this disease. This project is an attempt to build tools worthy of that mission.
